# The Quiet Revolution of Left-Handedness

For most of human history, being left-handed was treated as something between a quirk and a curse. The word "sinister" literally comes from the Latin for "left." Teachers routinely tied children's left hands behind their backs to force right-handed writing. As recently as the 1960s, this was standard practice in schools across Europe and America.

Yet left-handers make up a remarkably stable 10–12% of the human population — a proportion that has remained constant across cultures and centuries, even under enormous social pressure to conform. Evolutionary biologists think this persistence is a clue: being left-handed may confer advantages in combat or social contexts where opponents expect right-handed moves.

The brain wiring behind handedness is still not fully understood. Left-handers on average have more symmetrical brains, and some studies suggest greater cross-hemisphere communication. Whether that translates to genuine cognitive differences — or just centuries of left-handers having to think harder about living in a right-handed world — remains an open question.
