# The Surprisingly Dramatic History of the Semicolon

The semicolon (;) was invented around 1494 by Aldus Manutius, an Italian printer who wanted a symbol "weightier than a comma but lighter than a colon." He basically created punctuation drama for future programmers to argue about.

For the next 500 years, the semicolon lived a quiet life in literature — until programming languages conscripted it as a mandatory line-ender. Now it causes more heated debates than Oxford commas, tabs vs spaces, and whether `null` was a billion-dollar mistake (it was).

Fun twist: some languages like Python said "absolutely not" and banished the semicolon entirely, making it a kind of silent protest against C's legacy. The semicolon persists nonetheless, undaunted, punctuating code across the universe like a tiny, stubborn colon with commitment issues.
