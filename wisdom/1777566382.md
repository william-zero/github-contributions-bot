# The Strange Science of Deja Vu

Deja vu — that uncanny feeling you've lived a moment before — affects roughly 60-70% of people, yet scientists still can't fully explain it. The leading theory involves a brief misfiring between the brain's memory-familiarity circuits and its reality-checking systems, making a brand-new experience feel suspiciously like a memory.

Interestingly, people who travel frequently, watch lots of films, and are generally more tired report deja vu more often. Fatigue appears to loosen the brain's grip on the present moment, letting the "this feels familiar" signal slip through uninvited.

Rats may experience something similar: when placed in environments that partly resemble previously learned mazes, their hippocampal neurons fire in patterns eerily similar to the old maze — suggesting the brain's tendency to overmatch patterns might be ancient and deeply wired in.
