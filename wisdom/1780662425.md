# Goodhart's Law

## When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure

Named after economist Charles Goodhart, this principle was originally about monetary policy but has escaped into every corner of modern life. The moment you start optimizing for a metric, you decouple it from the thing it was meant to proxy.

Classic examples:
- **Soviet nail quotas**: factories given quotas by *number* of nails produced tiny, useless nails. Quotas by *weight*? One enormous nail, technically meeting the target.
- **Cobra effect**: British colonial India paid bounties for dead cobras to reduce the population. Locals bred cobras to collect bounties. Program cancelled → cobras released → more cobras than before.
- **Test scores**: Teaching to the test improves scores without improving the underlying knowledge the test was designed to measure.
- **GitHub contribution graphs**: *[no comment]*

The deeper issue is that metrics are simplifications of reality. Reality is always more complex than any single number. When you reward the number, you get the number — and often sacrifice everything else in exchange.

**The fix:** Use multiple metrics. Change them. Don't let people know exactly which metrics you're watching. And above all, stay skeptical when you see a metric that looks suspiciously perfect.
