# The Surprisingly Social Life of Trees

Trees in forests communicate through underground fungal networks called mycorrhizae — nicknamed the "wood wide web." Through these fungal threads, trees share nutrients, water, and even chemical warning signals. When a tree is attacked by insects, it can send distress signals that cause neighboring trees to preemptively produce defensive chemicals.

Old "mother trees" — the largest, oldest trees in a forest — actively send carbon and nutrients to younger seedlings, including their own offspring, favoring kin but supporting unrelated trees too. Ecologist Suzanne Simard discovered that trees recognize their own relatives and preferentially nurture them through this network.
