# the surprisingly accurate clock inside your stomach

Your gut has its own circadian rhythm, completely independent of your brain.
It tracks meal times so precisely that if you eat lunch at noon every day for
a week, your digestive system will start producing stomach acid at 11:55 AM
on day eight — even if you skip the meal.

Jet lag actually hits your gut harder than your brain. The internal clock in
your intestines can take up to two weeks to resync after a long-haul flight,
which is why food tastes wrong abroad even before you've tried the local cuisine.

The enteric nervous system (your gut's brain) contains roughly 500 million
neurons — more than your spinal cord — and produces about 90% of your body's
serotonin. So if someone tells you to "trust your gut," they're being more
neurologically accurate than they realize.
