# The Paradox of the Unexpected Hanging

A judge tells a condemned prisoner: "You will be hanged on one unexpected day next week. You won't know which day until the morning of."

The prisoner reasons: it can't be Friday — by Thursday night he'd know. Can't be Thursday either, for the same reason working backwards. By this logic, no day works — so the execution can't happen.

Then on Wednesday morning, the hangman arrives. Completely unexpected.

The paradox: the prisoner's logic was flawless, yet the judge was right all along.

Philosophers call this the **surprise examination paradox**. It reveals something strange about self-referential predictions and the limits of reasoning about one's own knowledge. The prisoner's chain of logic assumes the judge's statement is always true *and* always knowable — but that combination creates an impossible constraint that makes the whole system collapse.

Some things are only true precisely because you don't know them yet.
